# Output Quality

## Signal labels
Use labels a first-time reader can understand without reading the evidence. The label should say the behaviour, not a theme.

Good labels:
- Grounded public-service motivation
- Surface-level user probing
- UI craft could be sharper
- Changed wording without losing the principle
- Used constraints to narrow scope

Avoid labels that sound abstract, diagnostic, or consultant-like:
- Recovery under challenge
- Recovers well under challenge
- Handles challenge
- Insight origination unproven
- Idea ownership unproven
- Ability to keep two surfaces coherent
- Surface coherence
- Power dynamics
- Strategic range

If a label needs the evidence to make sense, rewrite the label.
Reject abstract-trait patterns like `[noun] under challenge`, `[noun] origination`, `[noun] dynamics`, or `[noun] coherence`. Name the behaviour instead.
Also reject near variants of bad labels. Changing `Recovery under challenge` to `Recovers well under challenge` is still a fail. Use the concrete behaviour instead, such as `Changed wording without losing the principle`.

## Evidence
Evidence should explain why the signal matters. Do not dump exact phrases, word-by-word negotiation, or roleplay trivia unless the detail changes the hiring judgement.

Write 2-4 short sentences for each strength or gap:
- what the interviewer saw
- why that behaviour raised or lowered confidence
- any limit on the signal, if the evidence is thin

Each bullet should include a plain meaning clause. This can be about the seat, the round, or the hiring judgement. Do not end with a detail dump.
One sentence is not enough unless the source note has only one sentence. If the source has more detail, add the plain meaning in a second sentence.

Bad pattern:
> The candidate changed one word, then another person clarified the concern, and both landed on another word.

Better pattern:
> She changed the wording when the concern was about the mental image, but kept the principle. This showed she could adjust detail without losing intent.

If the source is weak, say `Weak signal` in the bullet and do not use it as a main Round 3 probe unless it is central to the seat.
If the evidence text needs a hedge like "still legitimate", "not a specific example", "may not be indicative", "came in fast ideation", or "should not be overweighted", the bullet is a weak signal unless there is other strong evidence.

## Selecting gaps
Do not turn every open question into a gap. A gap is worth showing when it affects the hiring call or helps Round 3 know what to test next.

Use a gap when:
- prior feedback has concrete observed behaviour
- more evidence would change confidence for this seat
- it maps to the supplied requirements page

Omit or mark `Weak signal` when:
- the note is a single aside
- the behaviour came from a narrow roleplay moment
- the gap is niche compared with the seat needs
- the interviewer already warned not to overweight it

## Aspects to probe
An aspect is broader than one observed mistake. It should name a skill Round 3 can test through a real project story.

Good aspect headings:
- Ability to design against spoofing and misuse
- Ability to probe users for concrete examples
- Ability to make tradeoffs across citizen and agency needs
- Ability to improve shipped work after launch

Avoid aspect headings that overfit one moment:
- Ability to spot power and misuse dynamics in her own designs
- Ability to recover under challenge
- Ability to prove idea ownership

Each aspect must link:
- what previous rounds have not proven
- the concrete source evidence or missing evidence
- why the requirements page makes this worth probing

Keep aspects focused on high-signal gaps. Do not promote weak or niche gaps into main questions.
Before finalising each aspect, check: would this aspect still make sense if the reader did not know the exact prior-round moment? If not, broaden it, merge it into another aspect, or drop it.
Every aspect needs an explicit tie to the supplied requirements page. If the only support is a single roleplay moment, do not make it a standalone aspect. Mark it as a weak signal in prior-round evidence, merge it into a broader requirement-backed aspect, or omit it.
If a single weak moment touches a critical requirement, frame the aspect as missing positive evidence for that requirement, not as a proven candidate gap. Say `Weak signal` in the prior-round bullet, and write the aspect around the broader ability the seat needs. Do not make a weak single-moment signal the lead aspect unless the requirements page says that exact ability is the top hiring risk.
Do not add team leadership, growing designers, mentoring, or running critique as an aspect unless the supplied requirements page or a strong prior-round note says this Round 3 should test it. When included, frame it as design leadership for this seat, not generic management.

## Questions and Look for
Questions should test tradeoffs through real work where possible. Avoid questions that merely ask the candidate to defend a small prior-round moment.

The `Look for:` line should say what choices matter:
- what they chose
- what they gave up
- how they checked risk
- what would raise confidence
- what would lower confidence

Do not use vague words in `Look for:` unless they are tied to observable behaviour.
Each `Look for:` line must name a design tradeoff. Good tradeoff words include `between`, `instead of`, `gave up`, `kept`, `cut`, `risk`, and `why this was worth it`. If the line only asks for a trait, judgement style, or behaviour, rewrite it.
Research questions still need tradeoffs, for example speed versus depth, broad patterns versus recent examples, or asking more versus moving to synthesis.
Design leadership questions still need tradeoffs, for example helping versus taking over, raising the bar versus preserving ownership, or team speed versus designer growth.
